I mean, whole societies have justified violence for greater causes. And I think almost every act of violence that seems senseless to everyone has been justified at least to the perpetrator in some way, so understanding that, getting clarity on that, may be a fool's errand. A lot of the frustration, actually a lot of the impediments to healing and finding some kind of resilience, comes from not understanding why, and people get stuck there. ★ One thing I knew before December 2012 was that there are good journalists and bad journalists. There are people out there who want to cut corners, there are people who show up to report a story with the story already written in their heads. So I was cynical in some ways because of what I had seen. There's this pack pressure for journalists, and everybody just follows the same channel. And with the tragedy in Sandy Hook, all of that was turned up on steroids. ★ It became clear to me that for the preponderance of people who just came in to get the story, a lot of them had come to get a certain story that their editor had told them to get. So in order to file that story, they need X number of interviews, they need a person who will be an emblem of grief, they need a person who will be sobbing, they need a clergyman to talk about faith-all these components that they were assembling. And it didn't really matter who the person was, what their actual feelings were, the fact that they may feel aggrieved by being assaulted with questions that were so predictable, obviously intended to elicit a specific response, that there was great disillusionment among the townspeople. It was a great confirmation in my mind that there are ways where journalism goes completely astray and doesn't deliver the story; they deliver a narrative that's been concocted somewhere off-site from the story, and they just need the appropriate role players, and they will just go around until they find those. ★ There was a period- especially around the first anniversary, even around the six-month anniversary of it-the interest of the media reawakened, and they started coming to town, and there was a sense in the community that they didn't really want all the cameras and microphones back on the streets. So the town actually decided-the town leaders, with the input of clergy and other victims groups, and schools even-that maybe on the first anniversary there wouldn't be a community commemoration, but that people would observe it privately, go to their regular church, do what they normally would do. And actually word was put out: If you're coming to town looking for some pictures to take of this, you're not going to see anything, because nothing has been scheduled, above and beyond what we normally do.
